Motivation


Lack of proper English pronunciations is a major problem for immigrant population in developed countries like U.S. This poses various problems, including a barrier to entry into mainstream society. Project SPRING explores the use of speech technologies merged with activity-based and arcade-based games to do pronunciation feedback for Hispanic children within the U.S. A.



The solution

After some initial contextual inquiries we have realized that pronunciation should be treated as the focus for ESL acquisition in Hispanic children. We implemented speech-enabled versions of two popular games. The popularity of the games was deduced from demographic interviews with children at a high school.

Initial results

Initial results from a 3-month long user study with 20 children, can be summarized as follows:

  1. •Children who were part of the program showed statistically significant learning gains, as opposed to children who were not.

  2. •A linguistic evaluation of the pre-test and post-test data from the children corroborated the claim above.

  3. •Children showed similar learning gains, irrespective of common factors like gender and initial knowledge of the subject (pronunciations).



Future Work

Since initial feasibility studies suggest positive results and effects, we would want to look into the following areas in the near future:

  1. •Deployment with younger children.

  2. •Longitudinal deployments to study long term effects.

  3. •Wider range of speech-enabled applications like question/answering agents, shared reading agents etc.

  4. •Extension to mobile devices/platforms.
